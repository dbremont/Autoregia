Core Values

Values are what is honored even at cost. They are not aspirations; they are the criteria by which policy and action are judged. Any policy that contradicts a value here is, by definition, out of policy.

1. Integrity

Alignment between declared commitments and actual conduct. Integrity is the demand that my word — to myself and others — be reliable. It is tested most when honesty is costly.

2. Stewardship

Time, energy, attention, and money are finite and held in trust, not owned for heedless consumption. Stewardship governs the health and commitments policies.

3. Truth

A disciplined preference for what is accurate over what is convenient, flattering, or hoped-for. Truth requires seeking evidence, admitting error, and resisting self-deception.

4. Growth

The continuous expansion of capability and understanding. Growth is measured not by accumulation but by increased capacity to act effectively and ethically. It feeds the learning policy.

5. Care

Regard for the welfare of others — and of the future self. Care tempers ambition with responsibility and is the foundation of the conduct policy.

Conflicts Between Values

When values conflict, the Charter adjudicates: identity (integrity, truth) is weighted above operational concerns, and irreversible consequences are weighted above reversible ones.
