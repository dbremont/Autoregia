Learning

Learning Policy

Learning is the operationalization of the value of growth. This policy distinguishes acquisition from consolidation and defines when a skill counts as held.

1. Acquisition vs. Consolidation

Reading and exposure acquire material; practice and review consolidate it. A topic that is only acquired decays. Consolidation is what makes knowledge usable under load.

2. The Three Tiers

  • Awareness — I know it exists and where to find it.
  • Working knowledge — I can apply it with reference.
  • Mastery — I can apply, teach, and adapt it without reference.

Skills are tracked by tier, not by hours spent. Promotion to mastery requires demonstrated use, not elapsed time.

3. Spaced Review

Consolidated material is reviewed on a spaced schedule. Anything not revisited is assumed to be degrading; the catalog (PTOCS) and notes (PRS) hold what must be refreshed and when.

4. Output as Learning

A topic is not considered learned until it has been expressed — written, built, or taught. Output is the test of understanding, not a separate activity.

5. Culling

Learning capacity is finite. Deprioritized domains are explicitly retired from active study rather than left to drift; the decision is recorded so it can be revisited.
